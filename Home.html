Echoes of the Past

Origin

The history of robots dates back a long time. In 1498, Leonardo da Vinci designed a clockwork knight, marking the emergence of the early prototype of human-like machines. In 1920, the Czech writer Karel ÄŒapek published the science fiction play "R.U.R." (Rossum's Universal Robots), which is considered the origin of the term "robot."

First-Generation Robots: Teleoperated Robots

  • In 1947, the Oak Ridge National Laboratory in the United States developed the world's first teleoperated robot for handling nuclear materials. This was a remote-controlled device designed to perform tasks in environments too dangerous for human workers.
  • In 1962, the United States developed the Programmable Universal Machine for Assembly (PUMA), a general-purpose teach-and-repeat robot. These robots operate by following pre-programmed paths and actions stored in their memory. They are often used in repetitive tasks such as spot welding in the automotive industry, where the robot can be taught the task once and then repeat it indefinitely.

Second-Generation Robots: Sensory Robots

First-generation robots lack perception of their environment, meaning they cannot detect changes or make decisions based on feedback. To address this, sensory robots were developed in the late 1970s. These robots are equipped with sensors that give them a sense of touch, force, slip, vision, and hearing, allowing them to perceive and recognize the shape, size, and color of workpieces. This enables them to perform tasks with more autonomy and adapt to varying conditions.

Odes to Modern Robotics

The modern robots is the third-generation robots, also known as intelligent robots,and have been invented since the 1990s. These robots are equipped with a variety of sensors, capable of complex logical reasoning, judgment, and decision-making. They can autonomously determine their own actions in response to changes in internal states and external environments. These robots represent a new era of robotics technology, as they can not only perform preset tasks but also adapt to environmental changes, making self-adjustments and optimizations.

Embodied Intelligence and Vertical Large Model

Combining large models with embodied intelligence, along with the joint analysis of multimodal data such as text, images, and audio, more powerful and intelligent robotic systems can be created. This enables robots to better handle various situations and complex environments, and is expected to lead to well-scaled applications.

Humanoid and Quadruped Bionic Robots

Robots designed and manufactured by drawing inspiration from biological principles and structures, and by imitating the movement, behavior, and appearance of living organisms, can achieve more natural and adaptable performance.

Biomechatronics

Robotics technology is continuously integrating with other disciplines, leading to significant advancements in its applications within the medical field.
